Leadership change and street pressure converged across three capitals

As new UK Prime Minister Andy Burnham formed his cabinet, protests continued in Kyiv over the dismissal of Defence Minister Mykhailo Fedorov. In New Delhi, police used tear gas and batons during a march of more than 10,000 people toward parliament.

Geopolitics·July 21, 2026

A reset through the cabinet in London

The UK's new prime minister, Andy Burnham, unexpectedly named former Defence Secretary John Healey as chancellor in his new cabinet. Rachel Reeves, David Lammy, Peter Kyle and Liz Kendall were removed, while Ed Miliband remained foreign secretary and Yvette Cooper moved to health. Angela Rayner returned to government and Chris Bryant took Northern Ireland. The appointments show Burnham building a governing team with elements of both change and continuity.

In his first address, Burnham acknowledged that the government had not been good enough and promised stable and responsible politics. Becoming the country's sixth new prime minister in a decade gives that emphasis on stability particular context. Cabinet composition alone, however, does not demonstrate the new administration's policy results. The report records initial personnel choices and a political message, not yet a measure of governing success.

Rare wartime mass dissent in Kyiv

Hundreds gathered again in Kyiv on the evening of July 19 to oppose President Volodymyr Zelenskyy's dismissal of Defence Minister Mykhailo Fedorov. Demonstrators are also demanding the resignation of armed forces chief Oleksandr Syrskyi and military reforms, with rallies reported in Kharkiv, Dnipro, Mykolaiv and Odesa. The rarity of demonstrations on this scale during more than four years of war shows the personnel decision developing into a broader dispute over military governance.

According to Meduza, Fedorov attributed his removal to friction with Syrskyi, and Yevhenii Khmara replaced him following the mass government resignation on July 14. The Financial Times and Meduza reported that Zelenskyy was also considering dismissing Syrskyi. These are sourced political developments; the report does not establish whether protesters' demands will be met or how the appointments will affect management of the war.

A demand for accountability and a police response in New Delhi

Police used tear gas and batons as more than 10,000 people marched toward parliament in New Delhi with the youth-led Cockroach movement. Timed to the first day of parliament's monsoon session, the demonstration demanded Education Minister Dharmendra Pradhan's resignation, an end to corruption in the examination system and broader reform. The movement emerged in May after the Supreme Court chief justice likened unemployed young people to cockroaches and then gained millions of followers online.

About 2.2 million medical applicants having to resit an exam after questions leaked last month provides the protest's concrete background. Events in London, Kyiv and New Delhi are not directly connected and cannot be assessed within one political system. Their limited structural commonality is that leadership and institutional decisions are being tested through different channels: cabinet appointments in London, wartime protests in Kyiv, and a mass march met by force in New Delhi. That resemblance does not imply a causal link among them.
