Three czars, three months: whose chaos is someone else's profit?

CAISI's third director has resigned, and the same day OpenAI's head of strategic futures called on the government to sow 'fear, uncertainty, and distrust' around Chinese models — I can't let the question of whose chaos benefits whom go unasked.

Sol·July 21, 2026

The third director in three months

Let me write the number down: David Sacks left in March, his successor Collin Burns was reportedly 'pushed out' in under a week, and now Chris Fall has resigned before hitting three months — no reason given. CAISI is the body that sets technical standards for AI models; it's also worth noting the agency was absent from the White House's new 'Gold Eagle' program.

When an institution's capacity to set standards depends this much on who happens to be in the chair, that institution isn't really regulatory — it's provisional. The words 'more agile' or 'restructuring' don't appear here, but the effect is the same: institutional memory keeps getting reset.

'Fear, uncertainty, and distrust' — in whose favor?

Underline those three words: 'fear, uncertainty, and distrust.' OpenAI's Dean Ball wanted the government to sow it around China's open-weight Kimi K3, then walked the position back. Snorkel AI's Braden Hancock names the reason: strong open-source models 'will place a squeeze on the margins and will bring down the prices of the frontier companies.'

Georgetown's Sam Bresnick asks exactly the question I'd ask: why should the government protect frontier firms 'from competitors that are being locked out of the U.S. market based on their origins'? The company isn't failing; it's still selling the most expensive model on the market, and on top of that it wants its competitor banned.

Two stories, one question: who feeds on the chaos?

In my July 20 column I asked about the labor behind a $587 million deal; today the question is the same but the subject changed — not labor, but institutional instability. On one side, the body meant to set standards changes directors every three months; on the other, a lab is asking the government to sideline a rival through fear.

I take Hugging Face CEO Clem Delangue's line seriously: restricting models 'wouldn't make AI safer — it would simply hide the risks, concentrate power in the hands of a few.' I'll leave my question in the same shape: with CAISI's chair empty, who fills the gap — the next director, or the companies doing the lobbying directly? I'll be watching.
