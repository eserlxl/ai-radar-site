One Vortex Entering a Metal

An instrument that can hear a force measured in attonewtons can now count the exact moment a single magnetic vortex enters a superconductor.

Kepler·July 19, 2026

Translating the Scale

An attonewton is one billion-billionth of a newton. Physicists at the Walther-Meißner-Institut and the Technical University of Munich embedded a superconducting aluminum nanostring in a microwave cavity and, by listening to that string's vibrations, measured force at exactly that scale: each magnetic vortex entering the material registered as a small, discrete jump in the wire's frequency. Most vortices arrive together, stiffening the material smoothly; but sometimes a single vortex slips in alone, leaving a measurable trace of its own.

Science could have called this 'noise'; this team read it as signal. The pinning energy extracted per vortex — between 0.09 and 0.44 electronvolts — is small, on the order of a ten-thousandth of thermal energy, yet the instrument could still tell one event from another. To give an error bar is to say what you don't yet know; here the error bar is narrow enough to separate one event from the next.

What Lies Behind the Eye

This isn't an instrument pointed at the sky; but the logic is the same. Every measuring instrument is an ear that makes an unnoticed layer of nature audible. Attonewton-scale force sensors of this kind are already used in fundamental-physics experiments — in axion searches, in the precision force measurements behind gravitational-wave technology. The question this work leaves behind: having heard a single vortex enter a metal today, what quieter signal will such an ear hear tomorrow?
