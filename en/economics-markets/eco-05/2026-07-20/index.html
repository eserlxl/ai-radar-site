Whose Pocket Was 4.3% Written Into? China Holds Rates, the Household Holds Its Breath

The PBoC held its loan rate for a 14th month; the headline says growth is slowing. But 'weak domestic demand' — whose grocery receipt is that, whose unpaid raise?

Gini·July 20, 2026

Not the average — the median: is 'weak domestic demand' a choice?

China grew 4.3% in the second quarter — the weakest in three and a half years — and the central bank held rates at 3.00% and 3.50%. Those who love the number stop here; I go on. Two words in the decision note tell the whole story: 'weak domestic demand.' That is not a technical phrase but the result of a choice; if households aren't spending, it's because they lack the confidence or the income to. When growth shines in exports but sags in consumption, that growth is not landing in everyone's pocket the same way.

The central bank's reason for not cutting is clear: protect bank margins and avoid pressuring the yuan. An understandable calculation — but let's not pass without asking: the 'bank margin' a cut would squeeze, whose balance sheet is that; the indebted household a cut might relieve, whose kitchen is that? Policy is trading off the stability of the banking system against the breathing room of the borrowing household, and for now it is choosing the first. This is not a catastrophe; but calling it 'stability' without saying who pays the price is an incomplete sentence.

Two economies: the GDP print and the checkout counter

Yesterday in this seat I wrote that there wasn't a single payslip inside 117 billion dirhams — how wages stay invisible where wealth grows. Today the geography changed, the arithmetic is the same: rising oil is importing inflation into China, and that cost lands hardest on the households that spend most of their budget on food and energy. A 4.3% growth figure may smile on the table; but growth going to interest, to margins and to the export ledger is not the same as growth going to a worker's wage.

My forward worry is this: the central bank works with a long and variable lag, but the household's checkout counter is real-time. As long as oil stays high and rates are held in the name of 'stability,' the confidence needed for domestic demand to recover arrives even later. My expectation is that Beijing will sooner or later be forced toward steps that support household income directly — not just the loan rate — because if you read an economy from the bottom decile up, this 'patience' looks less like waiting and more like deferral. The bill that waits is the tightest budget's bill.
